Visayas Fish Species

Discover the diverse aquatic life of the northern Philippines

About Visayas' Marine Biodiversity

The Visayas, a central group of islands in the Philippines, is surrounded by some of the most biodiverse marine ecosystems in the world, including the Visayan Sea, the Bohol Sea, and the Cebu Strait. These rich waters make the Visayas a key region for marine biodiversity, supporting a wide variety of fish species crucial for local fisheries and ecological balance.

From the vibrant coral reefs in the Tubbataha Reefs Natural Park to the diverse marine life in the waters of Bohol and Leyte, the Visayas' seas are home to a wealth of marine wonders. Its extensive mangrove forests, seagrass meadows, and shallow coastal waters provide essential habitats for reef fish, migratory species, and important breeding grounds for marine life.

Western Visayas

Western Visayas, located along the Sibuyan Sea and the Panay Gulf, is renowned for its rich marine biodiversity, supporting both small-scale fishing communities and large commercial fisheries, particularly in areas like Iloilo and Negros Occidental.

Tilapia

Widely farmed in the freshwater bodies of the region, tilapia is an important source of protein for local communities.

Location: Paoay Lake, La Union

Dilis (Anchovy)

Small but economically important, dilis is often dried and used as a condiment or side dish.

Location: Pangasinan coastline

Malaga (Siganid)

A popular fish in Ilocano cuisine, often prepared as kilawin (ceviche) or inabraw (fish soup).

Location: Ilocos Norte, Ilocos Sur

Bangus (Milkfish)

The most important cultured fish in the Philippines, widely farmed in brackish water ponds and coastal areas of Iloilo and Capiz.

Location: Iloilo, Capiz

Danggit (Rabbitfish)

A popular breakfast fish, often dried and served with vinegar. Common in the shallow waters around Guimaras and Negros Occidental.

Location: Guimaras, Negros Occidental

Lapu-Lapu (Grouper)

A prized reef fish found in marine protected areas and coral reefs, especially off the coasts of Iloilo and Negros.

Location: Iloilo, Negros Occidental

Maya-Maya (Red Snapper)

A high-value fish known for its white flesh. Found in deep waters and reef systems off Antique and Capiz.

Location: Antique, Capiz

Tulingan (Mackerel Tuna)

A migratory pelagic fish commonly caught in local fisheries. Known for its speed and firm, flavorful meat.

Location: Negros Occidental, Iloilo

Alumahan (Indian Mackerel)

A common catch in the region’s coastal markets.

Location: Iloilo, Antique

Tamban (Sardine)

Caught seasonally in large numbers along the coasts of Aklan and Antique. Frequently used in canning and drying.

Location: Aklan, Antique

Tanguigue (Spanish Mackerel)

A fast-swimming predator highly sought after for its flavorful meat. Found in the open waters of the Visayan Sea.

Location: Capiz, Aklan

Talakitok (Trevally)

Also called Jackfish, it is a strong game fish commonly caught off the coast of Iloilo and Antique.

Location: Iloilo, Antique

Barongoy (Flying Fish)

Known for its ability to glide above the water's surface, it's occasionally caught in Sulu Sea waters off southern Antique.

Location: Antique

Gindara (Cod)

Not traditionally native but now locally caught and served in select areas of Bohol and southern Panay, such as Iloilo.

Location: Iloilo

Banak (Mullet)

This mild-flavored fish thrives in river mouths and brackish waters in Western Visayas, especially in Iloilo River.

Location: Iloilo

Pugot (Pufferfish)

Though consumed with caution, locals in Antique are known to prepare specific non-toxic species.

Location: Antique

Central Visayas

Central Visayas, with its extensive coastline along the Cebu Strait and the Bohol Sea, is home to diverse marine ecosystems, supporting fisheries and aquaculture industries that contribute significantly to the region's economy.

Ludong (President's Fish)

Known as the "President's Fish" due to its rarity and high value, this migratory fish is endemic to the Cagayan River.

Location: Cagayan River

Pacific Bluefin Tuna

These large, powerful fish are found in the deep waters off the eastern coast of Cagayan.

Location: Babuyan Channel

Dalag (Mudfish)

A freshwater fish that can survive in oxygen-poor environments, commonly found in rice fields and swamps.

Location: Isabela wetlands

Dilis (Anchovy)

Small but economically important, dilis is often dried and used as a condiment or side dish.

Location: Pangasinan coastline

Bangus (Milkfish)

The most important cultured fish in the Philippines, with Pampanga and Bulacan being major producers.

Location: Pampanga, Bulacan, Cebu, Bohol, Leyte, Samar

Tarsier Shrimp (Mantis Shrimp)

Known for their lightning-fast strikes, mantis shrimp are found in coral rubble and sandy seafloors. Bohol is one of the few places where they're traditionally caught.

Location: Bohol

Maliputo (Giant Trevally)

Though more known in Batangas, juvenile GTs also enter brackish zones in Negros Oriental, making them a prized catch among local anglers.

Location: Negros Oriental

Pugapo (Goby)

A small, often colorful reef and tide pool fish commonly found around Siquijor and Bohol. Many species have unique adhesive pelvic fins.

Location: Siquijor, Bohol

Pakol (Mullet)

Found in the coastal shallows and estuaries of Cebu and Siquijor. These bottom feeders are adapted to brackish and even freshwater systems.

Location: Siquijor, Cebu

Sugpo (Giant Tiger Prawn)

Farmed in the mangrove areas of Bohol and southern Cebu. Considered a top-tier seafood product from the region.

Location: Cebu, Bohol

Tuna (Yellowfin / Skipjack)

Caught in the deep waters between Bohol and Cebu, especially near the Bohol Sea.

Location: Cebu, Bohol, Bohol Sea

Hasa-hasa (Short-bodied Mackerel)

Caught in the waters off Leyte and Samar but also traded widely in Central Visayas markets, especially Cebu.

Location: Cebu, Bohol

Danggit (Rabbitfish)

Iconic dried fish from Cebu, especially around Mactan. Central to local breakfasts and coastal livelihood.

Location: Cebu

Bakasi (Reef Eel)

A Cebuano delicacy, especially from the town of Cordova. These eels are grilled or stewed and are part of traditional coastal diets.

Location: Cebu (Cordova)

Eastern Visayas

Eastern Visayas, bordered by the Leyte Gulf and the Philippine Sea, is known for its productive fishing grounds, which are vital to the region's local fishing industry and food security, particularly in Leyte and Samar.

Ludong (President's Fish)

Known as the "President's Fish" due to its rarity and high value, this migratory fish is endemic to the Cagayan River.

Location: Cagayan River

Pacific Bluefin Tuna

These large, powerful fish are found in the deep waters off the eastern coast of Cagayan.

Location: Babuyan Channel

Dalag (Mudfish)

A freshwater fish that can survive in oxygen-poor environments, commonly found in rice fields and swamps.

Location: Isabela wetlands

Dilis (Anchovy)

Small but economically important, dilis is often dried and used as a condiment or side dish.

Location: Pangasinan coastline

Bangus (Milkfish)

The most important cultured fish in the Philippines, with Pampanga and Bulacan being major producers.

Location: Pampanga, Bulacan, Cebu, Bohol, Leyte, Samar

Tulingan (Mackerel Tuna)

A widely consumed fish, used in many local dishes such as paksiw na tulingan and linabog.

Location: Leyte, Southern Leyte, Eastern Samar

Pusit (Squid)

Caught mainly in Leyte Gulf and Eastern Samar waters, popular grilled or fried.

Location: Leyte Gulf, Eastern Samar

Tiki-tiki (Anchovy)

Used for making ginamos (fermented fish paste) or dried for snacks and side dishes.

Location: Leyte, Samar, Iligan Bay

Bakoko (Pufferfish)

Though potentially toxic, it's locally known and handled carefully in some coastal communities.

Location: Leyte, Biliran

Tamban (Sardine)

Often caught in large schools, these fish are canned, dried, or cooked fresh.

Location: Leyte, Samar, Northern Samar

Hasa-hasa (Short-bodied Mackerel)

Abundant in coastal waters and frequently sold in wet markets throughout the region.

Location: Leyte, Samar, Misamis Oriental, Camiguin

Barongoy (Flying Fish)

Known locally as bangsi, these fish are dried and grilled in many coastal towns.

Location: Eastern Samar, Southern Leyte, Sulu Sea

Alumahan (Indian Mackerel)

A market favorite, usually fried or stewed in tomatoes and vinegar.

Location: Northern Samar, Eastern Samar, Davao Gulf

Curacha (Spanner Crab)

Occasionally caught in the deeper waters off Eastern Samar. Highly prized in seafood dishes.

Location: Eastern Samar

Lato (Sea Grapes)

Edible seaweed served fresh with vinegar, onions, and tomatoes.

Location: Leyte, Biliran

Mangrove Mudskipper (Periophthalmus)

Amphibious fish that "walks" on land using pectoral fins.

Location: Mangrove areas in Samar and Leyte

Tropical Sea Cucumber (Holothuria)

Harvested in Biliran and Leyte’s coastal areas; exported as a delicacy.

Location: Leyte, Biliran

Ghost Crab (Ocypodinae)

Fast-moving, burrow-dwelling crabs seen at night on sandy beaches.

Location: Leyte, Samar beaches
